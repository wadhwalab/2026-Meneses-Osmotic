# Biological Context

## The Biological Question

Cells must keep an electrochemical energy gradient across their membrane. In
bacteria this gradient is called the proton motive force, or PMF. PMF powers ATP
synthesis, flagellar motility, transport, and other essential processes.

This study asks what happens to PMF in _E. coli_ during the first seconds and
minutes after hyperosmotic shock: a sudden increase in external solute
concentration.

## Why _E. coli_

_E. coli_ is a well-studied bacterial model with powerful genetics, robust
growth, and a deeply characterized flagellar motor. It is small enough that
traditional electrical recordings are difficult, but its motor gives a useful
physical readout of membrane energy in living cells.

## Why Osmotic Shock Matters

Bacteria often encounter sudden changes in external osmolarity. When the outside
solution becomes more concentrated, water tends to leave the cell. This can
change cell volume, turgor pressure, membrane tension, and metabolism.

The key insight here is that osmotic shock is not only a mechanical or volume
stress. The data support the idea that it also causes a rapid, reversible loss
of membrane potential, meaning the energetic state of the cell changes almost
immediately.

## Why The Flagellar Motor Is Useful

The bacterial flagellar motor is powered by ion flow across the inner membrane.
For these experiments, motor speed is used as a real-time readout of PMF:

- faster rotation means stronger available motor-driving energy,
- slower rotation means weaker available motor-driving energy.

The bead assay attaches a microscopic bead to a flagellar filament. By watching
the bead rotate, the experiment measures motor speed with high time resolution.
This makes the assay well suited for the first few seconds after a shock.

## Why TMRM Is Included

TMRM is a fluorescent dye used as an independent membrane-potential indicator.
If both motor speed and TMRM fluorescence decrease after osmotic shock, the
interpretation is stronger: the motor slowdown is not just a motor-specific
artifact but is consistent with a broader membrane-potential drop.

## Why Viscosity Controls Matter

Adding sucrose or sorbitol makes the liquid more viscous. A bead rotating in a
thicker liquid should slow down even if the motor produces the same torque.

The paper therefore compares measured motor slowdown with a viscosity-only
prediction. The immediate slowdown is larger than viscosity alone can explain,
supporting the conclusion that PMF decreases during shock.

## Why The Control Conditions Matter

- Sucrose and sorbitol test whether the effect is specific to one osmolyte.
- Sodium phosphate buffer tests whether immediate potassium uptake is required.
- Clockwise-locked motors test whether the response depends on motor rotation
  direction or rotor conformation.
- Cell-area measurements show the physical shrinkage response that accompanies
  the energetic response.
- Sustained-shock adaptation data show that motor speed partially recovers over
  minutes, consistent with cellular adaptation and PMF restoration.

## Main Interpretation

Hyperosmotic shock rapidly depolarizes _E. coli_. The flagellar motor detects
this as a fast motor-speed decrease, TMRM supports the membrane-potential
interpretation, and longer recordings show partial recovery as cells adapt.
